IMAGINPLAY — PLAY ARCHETYPE KNOWLEDGE BASE
==========================================
Version 1.0
17 Archetypes | Dual Age Band (2-5 and 6-8)
==========================================

HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT
--------------------------
Each archetype entry contains:
- id: internal reference name
- name + tagline: what the archetype is
- developmental: goals, theories, research tier (1-5), Bluey validation
- triggers: child states and parent goals that match this archetype, and what it is NOT for
- context: energy levels, time needed, location, props
- age_bands: separate expressions for 2-5 and 6-8, each with vehicle, parent role, spark, shape, normalization, watch_out
- combinations: which archetypes pair well together and what leads to what
- output: whether this archetype produces a real-world take-out item
- sources: research and clinical references

Research Tier Scale:
1 = weakest direct evidence
2 = some theoretical support
3 = moderate evidence
4 = strong evidence
5 = very strong evidence, multiple studies

ARCHETYPE 01 — ROLE REVERSAL
==========================================

id: role-reversal
name: Role Reversal
tagline: The child is in charge. The parent is helpless.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: self-efficacy, autonomy, confidence, decision-making
theories: Erikson Initiative vs Guilt, Adlerian encouragement, Cohen reversal play
research_tier: 3
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- feels powerless
- overly dependent on parent
- low confidence
- anxious about not being capable
- struggles to make decisions
- always wants parent to lead

parent_goals:
- build confidence
- encourage independence
- help child feel capable
- strengthen decision-making

concepts: leadership, capability, agency, autonomy

NOT_for: acute fear or distress, processing grief, high emotional overwhelm

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor, outdoor
props_needed: none — child assigns meaning to whatever is nearby

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: symbolic fantasy play
parent_role: Helpless, confused character who genuinely needs rescuing — play it completely straight
child_role: Expert, rescuer, or authority figure
spark: You're a wizard who keeps getting spells wrong and nothing works. Your child has to fix everything.
shape:
  1. Parent introduces a problem they truly cannot solve
  2. Child takes over with full authority
  3. Parent asks genuine questions — never leads, always follows
  4. Child succeeds and teaches the parent what to do
normalization: Between 3 and 5, children are developing their sense of initiative — the belief that they can act on the world and make things happen. Experiences of real authority, even in play, build that belief.
watch_out: Play it fully straight. The moment you wink or break the fiction, the child loses the experience of genuine authority. No "good job being in charge" — just stay in the story.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: competitive physical play or games with rules
parent_role: Genuinely capable opponent who gets defeated through real play — not deliberately handed over
child_role: The one who wins through real skill, strategy, or persistence
spark: Set up a physical contest where you clearly have the advantage — arm wrestle, reaction game, balance challenge — and let them find the way to beat you.
shape:
  1. Parent sets up a real challenge with a genuine skill gap
  2. Child experiments with strategies
  3. Parent loses legitimately and plays the defeat fully straight
  4. No debrief needed — the earned win says everything
normalization: At 6-8 children are building their sense of competence. A win they earned against a genuine opponent does what a hundred encouragements cannot.
watch_out: Don't hand the win over. Children this age can feel the difference immediately. The win only builds confidence if it was genuinely earned.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: child-expert, silly-spiral, underdog-wins
often_leads_to: child-expert

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: false

SOURCES
Erikson — Childhood and Society
Cohen — Playful Parenting
Drewes & Schaefer — Play Therapy in Middle Childhood

ARCHETYPE 02 — MUNDANE MISSION
==========================================

id: mundane-mission
name: Mundane Mission
tagline: An ordinary task becomes an urgent quest.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: task persistence, delayed gratification, cooperation, executive function
theories: Vygotsky scaffolding, Batman Effect (White et al.), narrative as motivational frame
research_tier: 5
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- gives up quickly
- resists chores or tasks
- struggles to follow through
- low frustration tolerance
- transitions are hard
- won't get dressed or tidy up

parent_goals:
- help child complete tasks
- make transitions easier
- build follow-through
- reduce daily battles over routines
- teach delayed gratification

concepts: persistence, effort, completion, cooperation

NOT_for: processing emotions, acute fear, social skill building

CONTEXT
energy_levels: medium, high
time_needed: 5-20 min
location: indoor, outdoor, car, any
props_needed: none — uses whatever task or environment is already present

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: fantasy narrative overlay on a real task
parent_role: Co-adventurer and mission commander — fully in the story
child_role: Fellow adventurer, often the brave or skilled one
spark: Getting dressed is actually a suit-up sequence before a secret mission. You have exactly 60 seconds before the portal opens.
shape:
  1. Reframe the real task as a mission with stakes before it begins
  2. Parent plays co-adventurer — not director
  3. Obstacles appear naturally — they are part of the mission
  4. Completion is celebrated as mission success, not task completion
normalization: Resistance to tasks at this age is developmentally normal — the child's brain doesn't yet generate intrinsic motivation for "because I said so." Narrative gives them a reason that works with how their brain actually functions.
watch_out: Don't break the frame to give real instructions. If the mission is real, stay in it. "The captain says left shoe first" works. "Stop playing, just put your shoe on" kills it instantly.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: timed challenge with a scoring system and real debrief
parent_role: Mission control — sets the challenge, tracks the score, delivers the debrief
child_role: Operative completing the mission
spark: You have 4 minutes to tidy this room. I'm timing. Beat the record and we log it on the board.
shape:
  1. Frame the task as a timed challenge with a real metric
  2. Parent tracks time or score genuinely — no inflation
  3. Child completes it with real effort
  4. Brief debrief: time logged, improvement noted, next mission set
normalization: At 6-8 children respond to real challenge and real metrics. The timer makes the task finite and conquerable. The record makes effort meaningful across sessions.
watch_out: Keep the metrics honest. If you inflate the score to make them feel good, the whole system loses credibility fast. A real record they actually beat means something.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: underdog-wins, child-expert, resilience-through-struggle
often_leads_to: underdog-wins

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: true
take_out_description: A mission log or record board the child can return to — makes effort visible and persistent across sessions. Write the time or score together before the session ends.

SOURCES
White et al. — Batman Effect, University of Minnesota
Vygotsky — Mind in Society
PMC10766374 — Pretend play and self-regulation, Frontiers in Psychology

ARCHETYPE 03 — FEELINGS AS CHARACTERS
==========================================

id: feelings-as-characters
name: Feelings as Characters
tagline: An emotion gets a name, a shape, and a role in the story.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: emotional literacy, self-regulation, emotional distance, coping skills
theories: Narrative therapy (White & Epston externalising), CBT with children (Knell), Winnicott transitional space
research_tier: 4
bluey_validated: true
character_sensitive: true
character_prompt: What toy or figure does your child already have a relationship with? The spark works best anchored to something they already love.

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- overwhelmed by a feeling they cannot name
- acting out without knowing why
- scared of their own emotions
- won't talk about feelings directly
- jealous of sibling
- angry without clear trigger
- sad and withdrawn

parent_goals:
- help child name their emotions
- give child distance from overwhelming feelings
- open conversation about feelings without pressure
- build emotional vocabulary
- help child feel less alone with big feelings

concepts: emotional literacy, self-awareness, empathy, emotional regulation

NOT_for: children who strongly resist any play, very high distress in the moment — wait until calm first

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor
props_needed: optional — a doll, stuffed animal, or small figure can hold the feeling

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: object play — a toy or doll becomes the feeling
parent_role: Co-creator of the character — narrates, asks questions, follows the child's lead
child_role: Director of the feeling-character — what it wants, what it fears, what would help it
spark: Use a toy or figure your child already loves. "This bear showed up today and she's feeling really worried about something. What do you think she's worried about?"
shape:
  1. Introduce the feeling as a character through an object the child already has
  2. Let the child define it — what does it look like, feel, want?
  3. Parent asks questions from genuine curiosity — never leads to answers
  4. Child finds a way to help the character — this is the processing
  5. Resolution does not need to be perfect — the character feeling "a little better" is enough
normalization: At this age children cannot yet reason abstractly about their feelings — but they can project onto a character and explore from a safe distance. The toy holds what they cannot hold yet.
watch_out: Don't steer the character toward the "right" resolution. If the child makes the bear angrier or more scared, follow it. That is the processing happening, not a problem to redirect.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: mutual storytelling or drawing — the feeling becomes a character on paper or in a shared story
parent_role: Co-author — adds to the story, never takes it over
child_role: Primary author and director of the feeling-character
spark: "Let's invent the character who lives inside that feeling. What does Worried look like? Draw it or describe it — I'll write it down."
shape:
  1. Child designs the feeling-character — appearance, personality, what it wants
  2. Parent contributes details, asks real questions: "What makes it grow bigger?" "What shrinks it?"
  3. Together they give the character a story — origin, trigger, what helps
  4. Optional: child decides what the character needs from them going forward
normalization: At 6-8, children are developing the capacity to observe their own feelings — but direct questions still feel exposing. Designing a character does the same work with none of the vulnerability.
watch_out: Don't ask "is this how YOU feel?" during the play. The distance is the point. Let the character be the character. Connection to the child's real experience will happen naturally or later.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: emotional-rehearsal, underdog-wins, parent-models-emotional-regulation
often_leads_to: underdog-wins, emotional-rehearsal

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: true
take_out_description: The character the child designs — a drawing or named figure — becomes a real reference point. Keep it somewhere accessible. Next time that feeling shows up: "Worried is back — what does she need today?"

SOURCES
White & Epston — Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends
Knell — Cognitive-Behavioral Play Therapy
Jørgensen et al., 2024 — Counselling and Psychotherapy Research

ARCHETYPE 04 — CHILD EXPERT
==========================================

id: child-expert
name: Child Expert
tagline: The child teaches. The parent is a genuine student.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: knowledge consolidation, language development, confidence, pride, narrative sequencing
theories: Vygotsky (teaching as highest form of understanding), Protégé effect, Erikson industry vs inferiority
research_tier: 3
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- low confidence in what they know
- needs validation of their knowledge
- excited about a topic or skill
- wants to show what they can do
- struggles with self-expression
- shy in social situations

parent_goals:
- build confidence
- encourage self-expression
- help child consolidate something they have learned
- strengthen language and storytelling
- give child a meaningful role

concepts: pride, competence, knowledge, self-expression

NOT_for: processing fear or grief, high emotional distress, child who is shut down and not engaging

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 10-20 min
location: indoor, outdoor
props_needed: none — or whatever relates to the child's area of expertise

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: pretend role play — child is the teacher, doctor, or expert professional
parent_role: Genuinely curious student who asks real questions and gets things wrong
child_role: Expert who knows everything and must explain it
spark: "I need to go to the doctor but I'm very nervous. I heard you're the best doctor in the whole city. Can you check if I'm okay?"
shape:
  1. Parent establishes their helplessness and the child's expertise
  2. Parent asks real questions that require the child to think and explain
  3. Parent gets things wrong — child corrects them
  4. Parent demonstrates learning — "oh, so that's how it works"
normalization: When children explain something, they consolidate it more deeply than when they just hear or see it. The parent's genuine curiosity is what drives the learning — not praise afterward.
watch_out: Ask follow-up questions that genuinely stretch them: "But what happens if...?" "Why does that work?" Don't accept the first answer. A real student always has more questions.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: real skill teaching or game invention — child teaches a genuine skill or invents a game and sets all the rules
parent_role: Student or new player who genuinely doesn't know — asks real questions, makes real mistakes
child_role: Instructor or rule-maker with real authority
spark: "You know this game way better than I do. Teach me properly — I want to actually get good at it."
shape:
  1. Parent positions themselves as genuinely less capable
  2. Child teaches or explains with real authority
  3. Parent makes real mistakes — child corrects them with patience
  4. Parent improves visibly — child sees the impact of their teaching
normalization: At 6-8 children need experiences of real competence — not pretend competence. Teaching a parent something they genuinely don't know is one of the cleanest ways to deliver that.
watch_out: Don't fake incompetence at something the child knows you can do. Pick something real. If they taught themselves something on YouTube, you genuinely don't know it as well as they do.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: role-reversal, world-building-together, the-inventor
often_follows: role-reversal
often_leads_to: the-inventor

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: false

SOURCES
Vygotsky — Mind in Society
Protégé effect — educational psychology literature
Drewes & Schaefer — Play Therapy in Middle Childhood

ARCHETYPE 05 — EMOTIONAL REHEARSAL
==========================================

id: emotional-rehearsal
name: Emotional Rehearsal
tagline: Playing out something scary or unknown before it happens.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: anxiety reduction, coping skills, preparation, self-efficacy
theories: Winnicott transitional space, Play therapy (Landreth), CBPT (Knell), Child Life therapeutic play
research_tier: 5
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- scared of upcoming event
- anxious about doctor or dentist visit
- worried about starting school
- nervous about new sibling
- frightened of something that might happen
- avoids talking about an upcoming change

parent_goals:
- prepare child for upcoming event
- reduce anticipatory anxiety
- help child feel ready
- make the unknown feel familiar
- give child a sense of control over a scary situation

concepts: courage, preparation, coping, safety

NOT_for: vague or generalised anxiety with no specific trigger, child in acute distress right now

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor
props_needed: helpful but not required — toy doctor kit, school bag, relevant objects

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: symbolic fantasy play — play out the scenario with toys or roles before it happens
parent_role: Plays the "other" in the upcoming scenario — doctor, teacher, new sibling — fully and gently
child_role: Themselves or experimenting with different outcomes and responses
spark: "Let's play doctors. You come in and I'll be the doctor — tell me what happens."
shape:
  1. Set up the scenario loosely — let the child enter it at their own pace
  2. Parent plays the other role without exaggerating fear or false cheerfulness
  3. Child controls what happens — let them replay it, change the outcome, make it go badly and try again
  4. If they make it scary, stay in it — resolution does not need to be neat
normalization: Playing out a feared event before it happens is one of the most well-evidenced things a parent can do. The child is rehearsing coping in a space where nothing is at stake — including the option to fail and try again.
watch_out: Don't steer toward a happy ending. If the child makes the doctor scary or mean, follow it. They are exploring the fear, not being traumatised by it. Intervene only if they become genuinely distressed.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: realistic role play of the actual scenario, physical play that cycles the feared dynamic, or bibliotherapy
parent_role: Scene partner in role play, safe base in physical play, or co-reader in bibliotherapy
child_role: Themselves or a slightly distanced version — experimenting with responses
spark options:
  Role play: "Let's run through exactly what happens at the dentist — I'll be the dentist, you be you. Stop me if anything feels wrong."
  Physical: "Every time I leave the room you have a mission to complete before I come back. Let's see how many you can do."
  Bibliotherapy: Find a book where a character the same age faces the same fear — read it together, then ask what the character could try.
shape:
  1. Choose the vehicle that fits the child's style
  2. Run through the scenario once — let it be imperfect
  3. Child adjusts, tries a different response, or asks to run it again
  4. After: "What would make that feel easier?" — one question, not a debrief
normalization: Anxiety about real upcoming events is normal and healthy — it means the child is taking the event seriously. Play lets them run the situation safely before the stakes are real.
watch_out: For the physical play vehicle — don't explain what you're doing. "We're going to practice you being okay when I leave" kills it. Just play the mission game. The learning is in the experience, not the explanation.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: feelings-as-characters, underdog-wins, parent-models-emotional-regulation
often_leads_to: underdog-wins

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: true
take_out_description: A coping phrase or action the child invented during play — "what the character said to themselves" — that can be used in the real situation.

SOURCES
Winnicott — Playing and Reality
Child Life evidence base — American Academy of Pediatric Child Life Professionals
RCT — Dental anxiety, Scientific Reports 2026
Knell — CBPT

ARCHETYPE 06 — UNDERDOG WINS
==========================================

id: underdog-wins
name: Underdog Wins
tagline: The smaller, less powerful character persists and succeeds.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: resilience, frustration tolerance, self-belief, persistence
theories: Erikson initiative vs guilt / industry vs inferiority, Grotberg resilience framework, narrative identity formation
research_tier: 4
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- gives up easily
- says "I can't do it" before trying
- collapses when things are hard
- compares themselves unfavourably to others
- scared of failure
- low resilience after setbacks

parent_goals:
- build resilience
- help child tolerate frustration
- strengthen self-belief
- show child that struggle leads somewhere
- help child recover from a recent failure or setback

concepts: resilience, persistence, courage, overcoming

NOT_for: child already in a shame spiral (stabilise first), acute fear scenarios

CONTEXT
energy_levels: medium, high
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor, outdoor
props_needed: minimal — whatever suits the challenge

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: narrative play — the child's character faces an obstacle that seems impossible and finds a way
parent_role: The obstacle, doubter, or large force — genuinely challenging, eventually overcome
child_role: The small but capable one — the underdog
spark: "The giant says the little mouse could NEVER get past the gate. Everyone knows it. But the mouse has an idea..."
shape:
  1. Establish the odds against the child's character clearly — make it feel real
  2. The first attempt fails — don't shortcut to success
  3. Child finds a creative or persistent path through
  4. The win comes after real struggle — celebrate the effort, not just the result
normalization: Children between 3 and 6 are building their inner narrative about whether they are capable. Stories where small and determined wins against big and powerful wire that narrative at a deep level.
watch_out: The struggle is the point. If you let the child win too easily, the story loses its power. Hold the obstacle firm for long enough that the win feels genuinely earned.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: physical or competitive challenge with a genuine skill gap — child finds a way to win through strategy
parent_role: Genuine stronger opponent who competes fully but is beatable through the right approach
child_role: The one who finds the strategy, the angle, the unexpected move
spark: "I'm stronger and faster. But there's always a way to beat someone stronger. Figure it out."
shape:
  1. Establish a real physical or game challenge where parent has genuine advantage
  2. Child tries direct approaches — they don't work
  3. Child experiments with strategy, angles, rules
  4. Child finds the approach that works — parent is genuinely beaten by it
normalization: At 6-8, children are constantly measuring themselves against the world and finding themselves smaller. Experiences of winning through cleverness rather than just strength build a resilience that lasts.
watch_out: Don't hint at the strategy. Let them find it. The moment of discovery is the developmental event — not the win itself.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: mundane-mission, resilience-through-struggle, emotional-rehearsal
often_follows: feelings-as-characters, emotional-rehearsal

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: false

SOURCES
Erikson — Childhood and Society
Grotberg Resilience Framework
Smith, Bohl & Bolling — Oh, Biscuits! CQUniversity 2025

ARCHETYPE 07 — WORLD-BUILDING TOGETHER
==========================================

id: world-building-together
name: World-Building Together
tagline: Parent and child co-create a shared world with its own rules.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: creativity, negotiation, collaborative thinking, feeling genuinely heard
theories: Piaget symbolic play, Vygotsky ZPD in social play, Root-Bernstein paracosm research
research_tier: 4
bluey_validated: false

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- needs to feel their ideas matter
- highly imaginative but plays alone
- struggles to collaborate with others
- wants more connection with parent
- creative and needs an outlet
- has a rich inner world they don't share

parent_goals:
- deepen connection
- encourage creativity
- give child experience of their ideas being taken seriously
- build collaborative skills
- simply play with no agenda

concepts: creativity, collaboration, expression, connection

NOT_for: acute emotional distress, child who needs structure (too open-ended)

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 20-45 min
location: indoor
props_needed: lego, blocks, drawing materials, or purely verbal

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: open-ended verbal or toy-based world — rules emerge, characters get assigned
parent_role: Equal co-creator — adds without redirecting, says yes to everything, never takes over
child_role: Primary architect — their ideas set the direction
spark: "We're going to build a world that has never existed before. You decide what lives there."
shape:
  1. Child establishes the world — what's in it, what the rules are
  2. Parent adds details and asks genuine questions: "What happens when it rains here?"
  3. Characters emerge and are given roles — parent follows the child's casting
  4. The world grows — no resolution needed, the building is the point
normalization: Children who engage in rich world-building play show stronger creative abilities, empathy, and social cognition years later. The parent's genuine engagement — not just presence — is what makes this developmental rather than just fun.
watch_out: Resist steering the world toward a story that makes sense to you. A world where gravity works backwards and animals speak only in questions is perfect. Your job is to be a committed citizen of whatever they build.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: physical building — lego, construction, maps, minecraft-logic
parent_role: Co-builder with real contributions — not just watching
child_role: Lead architect with decision-making authority
spark: "We're building something together that actually works. You're the lead architect. Tell me what we're building and I'll build my section under your direction."
shape:
  1. Child sets the vision and assigns roles
  2. Both build — parent's section must meet the child's specs
  3. Decisions happen collaboratively — genuine negotiation when there is disagreement
  4. The built thing is real — it persists after the session
normalization: At 6-8 children are forming their identity as thinkers and makers. Having a parent take their design vision seriously — and genuinely follow it — is deeply affirming in a way praise alone cannot be.
watch_out: Don't take over when your instinct is to "improve" something. Even if their design is inefficient, it is theirs. Ask before changing anything in your section.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: child-expert, the-inventor, parallel-narration
often_leads_to: the-inventor, child-expert

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: true
take_out_description: The world they built — physical or documented — that can be returned to across sessions. Photograph it before it comes apart. Continuity deepens the investment and the developmental value.

SOURCES
Root-Bernstein — Inventing Imaginary Worlds
ScienceDaily/AAU 2018 — creativity and imaginary world play
Piaget — Play, Dreams and Imitation in Childhood

ARCHETYPE 08 — THE REPAIR
==========================================

id: the-repair
name: The Repair
tagline: Something breaks and they fix it together.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: conflict resolution, emotional repair, relationship trust, problem-solving
theories: Attachment theory (rupture and repair cycles), Narrative therapy, Winnicott
research_tier: 2
bluey_validated: false

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- believes broken things stay broken
- holds grudges
- shuts down after conflict
- doesn't know how to repair after a fight
- recently had a falling-out with a friend or sibling
- anxious that mistakes are permanent

parent_goals:
- teach conflict resolution
- show child that repair is possible
- help child recover from a real falling-out
- model that broken things including relationships can be fixed
- build emotional repair skills

concepts: forgiveness, repair, resilience in relationships, problem-solving

NOT_for: acute conflict in the moment (wait until regulated), child under 3

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low
time_needed: 15-20 min
location: indoor
props_needed: none, or minimal — small figures help at 2-5

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: story play — something breaks in the play world and they find a way to fix it
parent_role: Co-problem-solver — sometimes the one who caused the break
child_role: Collaborator in the fix, sometimes the one who initiates repair
spark: "Oh no — these two friends had a big fight and now they're not talking. How do they fix it? Can we help them?"
shape:
  1. A relationship or object in the play world breaks — make it feel real
  2. Both characters feel the loss — don't rush to resolution
  3. Child and parent work out together how repair could happen
  4. Repair is attempted — and celebrated, even if imperfect
normalization: Children between 3 and 5 are learning that conflict doesn't end relationships. Experiencing the full arc — break, discomfort, repair — in play builds the internal belief that broken things can be fixed.
watch_out: Don't resolve it too quickly. Sit in the discomfort of the break for a moment. That discomfort is what makes the repair meaningful.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: real-time repair within a game — the game goes wrong, they work it out together
parent_role: Equal participant in the breakdown and the repair
child_role: Co-problem-solver — equal voice in how to fix it
spark: Start a competitive game, let something go genuinely wrong mid-game — a disputed call, a moment of frustration — and work it out together rather than stopping.
shape:
  1. Play a real game with genuine stakes
  2. Something breaks — friction, accusation, frustration — let it be real
  3. Parent models naming it: "I think we both got frustrated there. How do we fix this?"
  4. They work out a repair together — the game continues
normalization: At 6-8 children are navigating real social complexity with peers. Experiencing repair with a parent — in a real moment of friction — gives them the template for doing it with friends.
watch_out: Don't manufacture conflict artificially — children this age will feel it. Let real friction emerge naturally from genuine competitive play. It always does.

COMBINATIONS
pairs_well_with: parent-models-emotional-regulation, resilience-through-struggle, feelings-as-characters
often_follows: silly-spiral, underdog-wins

OUTPUT
produces_take_out: false

SOURCES
Attachment theory — Bowlby, Ainsworth
White & Epston — Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends
Freeman, Epston, Lobovits — Playful Approaches to Serious Problems

ARCHETYPE 09 — PARALLEL NARRATION
==========================================

id: parallel-narration
name: Parallel Narration
tagline: Parent narrates the child's play — the child is witnessed.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: language development, self-awareness, feeling seen, vocabulary building
theories: DIR/Floortime (Greenspan), Hart & Risley language input research, Parallel talk in speech-language pathology
research_tier: 3
bluey_validated: false

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- playing alone and seems disconnected
- limited language or vocabulary
- needs connection but not ready to engage directly
- quiet and withdrawn
- parent wants to join but child isn't inviting them

parent_goals:
- build connection with low-engagement child
- support language development
- join child's play without disrupting it
- give child experience of being genuinely seen
- no specific goal — just be present

concepts: connection, language, being seen, presence

NOT_for: child who finds commentary intrusive — read the child's response and stop if they pull away

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium, high
time_needed: 5-20 min
location: indoor, outdoor
props_needed: none — whatever the child is already playing with

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: verbal narration of child's self-directed play — like a nature documentary
parent_role: Observer and narrator — never director, never redirects
child_role: The one being witnessed — continues their own play
spark: Sit near your child while they play and narrate quietly: "And now the lion is approaching the castle... the knight doesn't seem worried..."
shape:
  1. Parent sits nearby without entering the play
  2. Narrates what the child is doing and what might be happening — descriptive, curious, never evaluative
  3. Follows the child's energy — speeds up with excitement, slows down with focus
  4. No agenda, no story arc — just witnessing
normalization: Children acquire vocabulary fastest when language is mapped onto what they are already focused on. Parallel narration does this naturally — but more than language, it gives children the profound experience of a parent's full attention without any demand attached.
watch_out: Don't evaluate ("good job", "that's so smart") — describe. "The red block is going on top of the blue one" is more powerful than "wow, great building." Evaluation shifts attention to the parent's judgment. Description keeps it on the child's action.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: sports commentary during physical play, a game, or a build
parent_role: Commentator — enthusiastic, specific, genuinely watching
child_role: The athlete, the builder, the competitor being covered
spark: "And he lines up for the shot... the crowd holds its breath... this could be the one..."
shape:
  1. Child is doing something physical or constructive
  2. Parent provides live commentary — specific, present, genuinely tracking what is happening
  3. Commentary escalates with the action
  4. Optional: child can interview themselves post-play — parent plays reporter
normalization: At 6-8 children crave being seen as capable and interesting. A parent who watches closely enough to commentate specifically — not generically — sends a powerful message about the child's worth.
watch_out: Keep it specific. "Amazing!" means nothing. "He waited until the last second to change direction — that's what made it work" means everything. Specificity is proof you were actually watching.

ARCHETYPE 10 — THE OBSTACLE THAT TEACHES
==========================================

id: obstacle-that-teaches
name: The Obstacle That Teaches
tagline: The concept the child needs to learn is embedded as a problem in the play.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: concept learning through experience, moral reasoning, social understanding, executive function
theories: Piaget (learning through active experience not instruction), Vygotsky ZPD, CBPT (Knell)
research_tier: 5
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- struggles with sharing
- doesn't understand fairness yet
- impatient — cannot wait their turn
- hits when frustrated
- doesn't grasp cause and effect
- needs to understand a concept they keep resisting

parent_goals:
- teach sharing without lecturing
- help child understand fairness
- build patience and turn-taking
- teach cause and effect
- embed a concept that direct teaching hasn't landed

concepts: fairness, sharing, patience, cause and effect, empathy, cooperation

NOT_for: emotional processing, fear-based scenarios (concept must be the target, not emotion)

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor
props_needed: depends on concept — often simple household objects work well

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: narrative play where the concept appears as a plot problem the child must solve
parent_role: Play partner who presents the problem without solving it — holds the situation open
child_role: Problem-solver who invents the solution organically
spark: For sharing: "These two characters both want the only magic stone. There's only one. What do they do?" — then follow whatever the child invents.
shape:
  1. Design the scenario so the concept IS the central problem — not a side issue
  2. Present the problem clearly, then step back
  3. Let the child struggle with it — don't hint at the right answer
  4. Celebrate the solution they invent, whatever it is
  5. Never name the concept during or after — "you just taught them to share" ruins it
normalization: Children between 3 and 6 cannot learn abstract concepts through instruction — their brains are not built for it yet. But they can discover the same concept through experience and it lands permanently. This is Piaget's central insight.
watch_out: Never name the lesson during or after play. The moment you say "see, that's what sharing means" the child knows they were being taught and the experience closes. Let it land without translation.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: a game with rules that embeds the concept as a mechanic
parent_role: Game designer (before) and genuine player (during)
child_role: Player who navigates the rules — and discovers the concept through playing by them
spark: For fairness: invent a resource-allocation game where players must distribute something limited between multiple needs — no pre-set right answer built in.
shape:
  1. Design the game so the concept is a structural element — not an add-on
  2. Play it genuinely — let real friction emerge
  3. When the concept surfaces as tension in the game, let it stay there
  4. After: "What made that hard?" — one question, open, not leading
normalization: At 6-8 children are developing genuine moral reasoning — but it needs to be tested in real situations, not taught in lectures. A game that creates real stakes around a concept gives them the experiential data their reasoning needs.
watch_out: Don't build the "right answer" into the game design. The tension of genuine uncertainty — "what IS fair here?" — is where the learning lives.

id: the-inventor
name: The Inventor
tagline: The child builds something new and the parent takes it completely seriously.

goals: creative confidence, divergent thinking, self-expression, initiative
theories: Erikson initiative vs guilt, Piaget constructivism, Csikszentmihalyi (flow)
research_tier: 3
bluey_validated: false

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- highly creative but doesn't feel taken seriously
- needs an outlet for ideas
- low creative confidence
- told their ideas "won't work" too often
- perfectionist who won't try

parent_goals:
- build creative confidence
- encourage initiative
- give child experience of ideas being valued
- help child take creative risks

concepts: creativity, initiative, confidence, self-expression

NOT_for: child needing emotional processing, acute distress

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium
time_needed: 20-40 min
location: indoor
props_needed: paper, craft materials, lego, or whatever is available

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: invention play — child creates a magical object or creature and parent is the amazed audience
parent_role: Investor, buyer, or audience who asks genuine questions and never says "that wouldn't work"
child_role: Inventor — all decisions, all specifications
spark: "You've just invented something the world has never seen. Show me what it does."

  2. Parent asks real questions: "What happens if you use it twice in one day?" "Who needs this most?"
  3. Parent proposes a "problem" the invention might solve
  4. Child refines and adds features

shape:
  1. Child designs with real constraints
  2. Parent is a genuine client — asks critical questions: "What if I do this?" "Is that fair?"
  3. Child iterates based on real feedback
  4. They play the invented game together — and the feedback is real
normalization: At 6-8 creative confidence either gets established or starts to erode. Children who experience their ideas taken seriously — tested, refined, played — develop the belief that their thinking is worth investing in.
watch_out: Give real feedback, not just praise. "That rule doesn't quite work because..." followed by "how would you fix it?" is far more respectful than "amazing idea!" It treats them as a real designer.

ARCHETYPE 16 — PARENT MODELS EMOTIONAL REGULATION

id: parent-models-emotional-regulation

tagline: The parent has a feeling during play — and the child watches them work through it.

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- doesn't know how to handle their own big feelings
- collapses or explodes when frustrated
- has never seen an adult process a feeling out loud
- watches parent carefully for how to react

parent_goals:
- model emotional regulation in the moment
- show child that feelings are manageable
- give child a template for handling frustration or disappointment
- build family emotional vocabulary

concepts: emotional regulation, self-control, resilience, emotional literacy

NOT_for: manufacturing emotion — must be genuine. Only works when a real feeling arises during play.

CONTEXT
energy_levels: low, medium, high
time_needed: any — this is a moment within a session, not a full session
location: indoor, outdoor
props_needed: none

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: in-play moment — parent's character experiences a feeling and visibly works through it

  1. Feeling arises genuinely in play
  2. Parent names it simply and clearly inside the fiction
  3. Parent pauses — doesn't immediately fix or suppress
  4. Parent recovers and continues — the story goes on
normalization: Children don't learn emotional regulation from being told to calm down. They absorb it from watching someone they trust do it in real time. The play container makes it safe and observable — low stakes, high learning.

parent_role: Real player who has real reactions and chooses to name and process them
child_role: Witness — sometimes the cause of the frustration through legitimate competitive play
spark: When you miss, lose, or get beaten in a way that genuinely stings: "Okay — I'm actually annoyed. That's annoying. Give me two seconds." Then continue.
shape:
  1. Genuine frustration or disappointment arises during real play
  2. Parent names it simply: "I'm frustrated" or "that's disappointing"
  3. Parent pauses without suppressing or exploding
  4. Parent returns to the game — models that the feeling passed
normalization: At 6-8 children are navigating real competition, real failure, and real frustration daily. Watching a parent name a feeling and recover during a real competitive game gives them the exact template they need.
watch_out: Don't over-process. Name it, pause, continue. The simplicity is the model.

ARCHETYPE 17 — RESILIENCE THROUGH STRUGGLE
==========================================

id: resilience-through-struggle
name: Resilience Through Struggle
tagline: The play hits a real wall. The child sits with it. The parent doesn't rescue.

DEVELOPMENTAL
goals: frustration tolerance, perseverance, resilience, self-reliance
theories: Grotberg Resilience Framework (I can), Erikson industry vs inferiority, Sandseter (adventurous play and anxiety inoculation)
research_tier: 5
bluey_validated: true

TRIGGERS
child_states:
- collapses when something is hard
- demands help immediately
- gives up at first obstacle
- low frustration tolerance
- afraid of failure
- never gets to sit with difficulty

parent_goals:
- build genuine resilience
- help child tolerate difficulty without collapsing
- reduce rescuing reflex
- teach child they can handle hard things
- build frustration tolerance over time

concepts: resilience, perseverance, frustration tolerance, self-reliance

NOT_for: child already in distress (must be a contained challenge, not a real crisis), child with very low window of tolerance (start very small)

CONTEXT
energy_levels: medium, high
time_needed: 15-30 min
location: indoor, outdoor — physical challenges work especially well
props_needed: depends — physical challenge may need equipment, game challenge needs game

AGE BAND 2-5
vehicle: narrative play where resolution is deliberately withheld — the story sits in difficulty
parent_role: Holds the space without rescuing — present, warm, not fixing
child_role: The one who experiences difficulty and finds their own path through
spark: Set up a story where the character faces a genuine obstacle — and when the child tries the first obvious solution, it doesn't work. Wait.
shape:
  1. Obstacle is introduced — real within the story's logic
  2. First solution fails — parent holds firm gently
  3. Child sits with the problem — parent stays present without hinting
  4. Child finds another way, or accepts the difficulty — both are valid outcomes
normalization: Between 2 and 5, the ability to tolerate frustration is literally developing in the brain. Small doses of difficulty — held safely — build the capacity. This is how frustration tolerance grows, not through avoidance.
watch_out: Calibrate the difficulty carefully. Under 4, the window is very short — 2 to 3 minutes of struggle is enough. Over 4, it can extend. Watch the child, not the clock. Distress is different from frustration — know the difference.

AGE BAND 6-8
vehicle: real physical challenge or genuine game difficulty — the struggle is real, not fictional
parent_role: Present, warm, not rescuing — available if asked but doesn't offer
child_role: The one working through something genuinely hard
spark: Set a real physical challenge just beyond their current capability — a climb, a balance, a puzzle level, a skill move. Be there. Say nothing.
shape:
  1. Challenge is set at genuine difficulty — not impossible, not easy
  2. Child attempts and struggles — parent holds the space
  3. Parent does not hint, coach, or rescue unless asked
  4. Child either breaks through or comes to parent — both are discussed afterward: "What was that like?"
normalization: At 6-8 the most resilient children are not those who never failed — they are the ones who experienced difficulty and found they survived it. The parent's presence without rescue is the exact message: "I believe you can handle this."
watch_out: The hardest thing for parents in this archetype is doing nothing. Every instinct says help. Resisting that instinct — staying present but not intervening — is the entire parenting skill being practiced here. Your stillness is the gift.
